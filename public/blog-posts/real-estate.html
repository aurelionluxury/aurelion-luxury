Asset Autopsy — I

Oil: The Mirage of Strategic Value

Oil is the lifeblood of industrial warfare — and precisely because of this, it becomes the first casualty of geopolitical conflict. When war erupts, oil infrastructure becomes a prime military target. Pipelines are bombed. Refineries are seized. Shipping lanes are blockaded. The Strait of Hormuz, through which nearly a fifth of the world's oil supply passes, transforms from a trade corridor into a strategic chokepoint overnight.

For the individual investor, exposure to oil in a world war scenario carries compounding dangers: price volatility that can swing 80% within weeks, potential nationalisation of energy assets by state actors, and the complete collapse of settlement mechanisms when global financial infrastructure fractures. The 1973 Arab Oil Embargo, the Gulf War's market disruptions, and Russia's weaponisation of gas supply post-2022 all illustrate one irrefutable truth — oil is a geopolitical weapon, not a safe investment harbour.

Furthermore, the accelerating global pivot to renewable energy means that oil's long-term investment thesis is being structurally dismantled. In a world war, you may see a temporary spike — but the aftermath, including sanctions, market restructuring, and the green transition, makes oil a speculator's game, not a wealth preserver's choice.

Asset Autopsy — II

Stocks: Paper Wealth on a Burning Map

The relationship between war and equity markets is historically unambiguous: announcements of major conflict trigger immediate, severe market crashes. When Germany invaded Poland in September 1939, the London Stock Exchange suspended trading for weeks. When the United States entered World War II after Pearl Harbour, the Dow Jones saw catastrophic sessions. In more recent memory, global equities shed trillions in hours when Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022.

But beyond the initial crash lies a more insidious threat: currency debasement and hyperinflation. Wars are financed by printing money. Weimar Germany's hyperinflation of 1923, which emerged directly from war debt monetisation, rendered stock portfolios worthless in real terms even as nominal values soared. When the unit of account itself collapses, equity ownership becomes a mathematical illusion.

Additionally, multinational corporations — the backbone of major indices — face existential risks in total war: supply chain destruction, forced nationalisation, sanctions cutting off revenue streams, and key personnel losses. Stocks are, at their core, claims on future corporate earnings. In a world at war, that future becomes profoundly uncertain.

"Equities are a promise about tomorrow. Real estate is a fact about today. In wartime, the market trades in promises. Landowners deal in facts."

Asset Autopsy — III

Gold: The Ancient Safe Haven with Modern Flaws

Gold's reputation as the ultimate crisis asset is one of history's most enduring narratives — and it contains genuine merit. Gold is stateless, globally recognised, and has maintained purchasing power across five thousand years of human civilisation. In the early phase of geopolitical escalation, institutional investors pile into gold, prices surge, and the metal fulfils its safe-haven promise.

But history reveals gold's darker wartime chapter. In 1933, the United States government issued Executive Order 6102, making it illegal for American citizens to own gold bullion, forcing confiscation at fixed prices well below market rates. Similar confiscations occurred across Europe during World War II. Governments at war require total resource mobilisation — and gold in private hands represents a reserve they will claim.

Beyond confiscation risk, physical gold generates zero income, requires secure (costly) storage, and faces severe liquidity constraints in a full-scale conflict when physical movement of assets becomes dangerous or impossible. Gold ETFs and digital gold carry counterparty risk — when the financial system itself is under siege, the intermediary holding your gold may cease to function. Gold is better than nothing; it is not better than land.

The Case for Real Estate

Land: The Asset Even War Cannot Destroy

Real estate's fundamental advantage in any crisis scenario derives from a single irreducible fact: land is finite, immovable, and universally needed. Unlike oil reserves that can be bombed, stock certificates that can become worthless, or gold that can be confiscated, real property in stable geographies retains both physical and economic substance.

Consider the trajectory of prime real estate in major conflict-adjacent economies. India, positioned as a geopolitical swing state and economic growth engine, witnessed residential property values in Mumbai's premium corridors continue appreciating through the post-9/11 crisis, the 2008 financial collapse, the COVID pandemic, and ongoing Russia-Ukraine market shocks. The Worli skyline did not care about geopolitical headlines.

06

Premium real estate in cities like Mumbai shows near-zero correlation with global equity markets. When Sensex crashes 30%, your Worli apartment does not follow it down.

Why India's Financial Capital
Is The Geopolitical Safe Zone

India's strategic positioning in a world war scenario is uniquely favourable. As the world's largest democracy, a nuclear power, and a nation with deep diplomatic relationships across both the Western and Eastern blocs, India is unlikely to be a theatre of direct conflict in any foreseeable geopolitical escalation. This non-alignment heritage, now reframed as "strategic autonomy," insulates Indian assets from the direct destruction risk that confronts European, Middle Eastern, or East Asian property.

Mumbai specifically — as India's financial capital, home to SEBI, BSE, the Reserve Bank of India, and the headquarters of virtually every major Indian and multinational corporation — represents a city whose economic relevance transcends any single geopolitical cycle. The Worli-Bandra corridor, South Mumbai's heritage precincts, and the emerging luxury nodes of the Western Suburbs are not merely real estate locations. They are positions within one of the world's most resilient economic ecosystems.

Furthermore, India's demographic dividend — a young, urbanising, income-rising population of 1.4 billion — generates structural demand for premium housing that operates entirely independently of global war cycles. Even as capitals in Europe or the Middle East face existential uncertainty, Mumbai's luxury residential absorption has maintained positive momentum across every major global crisis of the past three decades.
